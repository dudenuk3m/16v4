Clinical insight

In helical acquisition, the CT table advances continuously through the patient while data is collected. Over a 10-second scan window, the contrast bolus does not remain static — it continues to arrive, peak, and wash out from the vasculature while the table is still travelling. The cranial slices are acquired early, capturing the organ at or near peak arterial enhancement. The caudal slices are acquired several seconds later, by which point the bolus has redistributed into the venous phase and parenchymal enhancement has begun to decline.

This temporal offset across the Z-axis produces an enhancement gradient: structures at the top of the coverage volume appear brighter than those at the base — not because of true anatomical difference, but because they were imaged at different moments on the time-attenuation curve. In cardiac CT, this can mimic or obscure true perfusion defects. In liver imaging, it can distort arterial-phase characterisation of focal lesions. The ΔHU figure above quantifies this gradient directly.

Clinical insight

Wide-detector volume CT acquires the entire coverage range in a single, stationary gantry rotation — typically 0.28 seconds. Because the table does not move during data collection, every slice from the apex to the base of the coverage volume is acquired at the same instant on the bolus time-attenuation curve. The result is isochronous enhancement: cranial and caudal structures share an identical contrast phase.

This temporal uniformity is clinically consequential. In cardiac CT perfusion, it allows true myocardial blood volume maps to be derived without motion or phase artefact. In hepatic and renal imaging, it enables reliable arterial-phase capture of hypervascular lesions that would otherwise be missed or partially visualised by a table-traversing acquisition. The ΔHU figure — effectively zero — reflects the absence of any bolus-driven enhancement gradient across the imaged volume.
